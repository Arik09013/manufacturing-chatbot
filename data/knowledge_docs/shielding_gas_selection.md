# Shielding gas selection for MIG/MAG (GMAW)

The shielding gas protects the molten weld pool from atmospheric oxygen and
nitrogen, and strongly affects arc stability, penetration profile, spatter,
and bead appearance. Gas choice depends on the base material, the desired metal
transfer mode, and the joint.

## Carbon and mild steel

Pure CO2 gives deep penetration and is cheap, but runs a harsh, globular arc
with high spatter and only supports short-circuit and globular transfer. An
argon-rich mix such as 80% Ar / 20% CO2 (often written Ar-20%CO2 or "C20")
gives a smoother arc, less spatter, and supports spray transfer at higher
currents. Mixes with lower CO2 (e.g. Ar-8%CO2) reduce spatter further and are
common for thin sheet and robotic welding. Adding a few percent oxygen
(Ar-1-2%O2) stabilises the arc on spray transfer.

## Stainless steel

Stainless is typically welded with a tri-mix: argon with a small CO2 or oxygen
addition plus, in some mixes, helium — for example Ar / 2% CO2, or a
He / Ar / CO2 tri-mix. Keep the CO2 content low (≤ 2-3%) because carbon pickup
from CO2 raises the carbon content of the weld and can sensitise the heat-
affected zone, hurting corrosion resistance. High-CO2 mixes also raise porosity
risk on stainless. Helium additions increase heat input and travel speed on
thicker stainless sections.

## Aluminium

Aluminium is welded with pure argon, or argon-helium mixes for thicker
sections. Never use any CO2 or oxygen on aluminium — they oxidise the pool and
cause porosity and dross. Helium raises heat input, which helps on thick plate
where aluminium's high thermal conductivity pulls heat away from the joint.
Clean the oxide layer and degrease before welding to control hydrogen porosity.

## Flow rate and practical notes

Typical MIG shielding-gas flow is 15-20 L/min; too low under-protects the pool
(porosity), too high causes turbulence that draws in air. Increase flow for
larger nozzles, longer stick-out, and draughty environments, and shield the arc
from wind. A leaking gas line, blocked diffuser, or damaged nozzle starves the
shield and is a common hidden cause of porosity even when the flow gauge reads
correctly.
